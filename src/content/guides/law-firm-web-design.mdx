## Introduction

I've reviewed hundreds of law firm websites. The pattern is always the same: a beautiful, professionally designed site that generates almost zero leads. Partners spent $8,000 to $15,000 on redesigns. They have polished photography, elegant fonts, and smooth animations. Yet the phone barely rings.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: a beautiful law firm website that doesn't convert is just expensive decoration.

Most law firms treat web design like a checkbox on a business plan. They hire a designer, get something that looks good, launch it, and expect results. When those results don't come, they assume the website is fine—the problem must be marketing or business development.

That's backwards thinking.

Your website is your most important marketing asset. It's available 24/7, it's your first impression for most prospects, and it directly impacts whether someone calls your firm or hires a competitor. A non-converting website doesn't just waste money—it actively harms your growth.

The difference between a law firm website that generates leads and one that doesn't isn't usually about design aesthetics. It's about strategy, psychology, and conversion optimization. It's about understanding what potential clients actually need to see to take action.

In this guide, I'm going to show you exactly why most law firm websites fail and what actually makes them work. I'll share the specific features, structures, and strategies that turn website visitors into qualified leads and clients. By the end, you'll understand what your firm's website should do—and whether it's currently doing it.

## Why Most Law Firm Websites Fail

Let me be direct: most law firm websites fail because they're designed for lawyers, not for clients.

I see this constantly. Firms create websites that showcase their accomplishments, their credentials, their case results, and their impressive bios. The firm name is prominently displayed. The partners' faces are featured. The office looks professional.

From the firm's perspective, this makes sense. You're proud of your accomplishments. You want prospects to know about your experience.

But here's what happens on the client's side: they land on your website scared, confused, and skeptical. They're facing a legal problem that's stressing them out. They need answers. They need confidence that you can solve their problem. They're not impressed by your office or your law school credentials.

Instead of addressing their concerns directly, most law firm websites force visitors to navigate through pages of firm history, partner bios, and generic information to find out: What does this firm actually do? Do they handle cases like mine? What happens next if I contact them?

**The Design vs. Conversion Problem**

There's a fundamental conflict that most law firm website projects never resolve: design aesthetics versus conversion psychology.

A beautiful website often conflicts with a converting website. Here's why:

A designer's job is to create something visually pleasing, unique, and memorable. Conversion optimization focuses on clarity, credibility, and psychological triggers that encourage action. These goals aren't always aligned.

For example, a minimalist design with lots of whitespace might look elegant, but it can leave visitors unsure about what to do next. A designer might choose a beautiful serif font that looks professional but isn't as readable on mobile devices. A firm might want animated hero sections because they're trendy, but animations can distract from the actual message.

The firms winning with web design are the ones who prioritize conversion, then make it look good. Not the other way around.

**The Hidden Conversion Killers**

Beyond the design-versus-conversion problem, specific issues destroy conversion rates on law firm websites:

First, lack of clarity about specialization. Generic websites that claim to handle "corporate law, litigation, real estate, employment, and family law" confuse visitors. Someone with an employment law question can't tell if this is the right firm. They click away.

Second, missing trust signals. Potential clients want evidence that you're legitimate and good at what you do. Client testimonials, case results, bar associations, certifications, and press mentions build that trust. Most law firm websites are missing these entirely or bury them on obscure pages.

Third, friction in the contact process. If someone decides they want to speak with your firm, how easy is it actually to reach you? Is your phone number visible on every page? Is your contact form simple or does it ask 20 questions? How fast do you respond to inquiries?

Fourth, poor mobile experience. Over 50% of law firm website traffic now comes from mobile devices. If your site doesn't look professional and function smoothly on phones, you're losing half your potential clients before they even learn about your firm.

Fifth, no clear next steps. Visitors should always know what happens next. Is it calling? Filling out a form? Scheduling a consultation? If your next steps aren't clear, many visitors will assume they need to do nothing and simply leave.

Sixth, outdated content. A website with a blog post from 2019 looks dead. Outdated testimonials or case studies make prospects wonder if your firm is still active. Fresh, relevant content signals that you're current and engaged.

The firms that convert website visitors into clients have systematically addressed every one of these problems.

## What a Law Firm Website Should Cost

Before we dive into what makes a website work, let's address a question I hear constantly: How much should my law firm website cost?

The answer, frustratingly, is: it depends. But I can give you a framework.

Website costs vary dramatically based on what you're actually building. Many firms compare prices without understanding what they're actually comparing. They see a $4,000 quote and a $25,000 quote and assume one is being overcharged.

That's wrong. They're usually comparing two completely different things.

**The Cost Breakdown**

Here's what you should expect:

| Website Type | Cost Range | Best For |
|--------------|------------|----------|
| Template/DIY | $500-$2,000 | Solo practitioners, starting out |
| Custom Basic | $3,000-$8,000 | Small firms (2-5 attorneys) |
| Custom Mid-tier | $8,000-$20,000 | Growing firms (5-20 attorneys) |
| Custom Premium | $20,000-$50,000+ | Established firms, complex needs |

**Template/DIY ($500-$2,000)**

These are Wix, Squarespace, or WordPress with pre-built legal templates. You or a freelancer customize them with your information.

Pros: Cheap, fast to launch, easy to update.
Cons: Everyone's site looks similar, limited customization, no optimization for conversion, poor SEO foundation, limited integrations with your practice management system.

Appropriate for: Solo practitioners on a tight budget who understand this is a temporary solution. Not appropriate for any firm that's serious about growth.

**Custom Basic ($3,000-$8,000)**

A small design agency or experienced freelancer builds a custom site from scratch. Usually includes basic brand strategy, 4-6 main pages, contact form, and integration with your email or CRM.

Pros: Custom design, professional look, decent SEO foundation, some conversion optimization.
Cons: Limited pages, basic features, may lack advanced functionality, designer may not specialize in law firm conversion.

Appropriate for: Small firms with 1-5 attorneys that want a professional presence without extensive investment.

**Custom Mid-tier ($8,000-$20,000)**

A specialized agency handles strategy, design, development, conversion optimization, and possibly content creation. Usually includes 8-12 pages, multiple custom features, SEO optimization, and testing.

Pros: Professional conversion strategy, comprehensive site, better integration, ongoing optimization support.
Cons: More expensive, longer development timeline, requires more input from your firm.

Appropriate for: Growing firms that take their digital presence seriously and understand that investment in conversion optimization pays back through more qualified leads.

**Custom Premium ($20,000-$50,000+)**

Full-service agency with strategy, design, custom development, conversion optimization, content strategy, and implementation. Includes advanced features, integrations, animations, and ongoing optimization.

Pros: Entire strategic approach, sophisticated user experience, advanced features, full integration with other business systems, ongoing support and optimization.
Cons: Expensive, longer timeline, requires significant firm input, overkill for most firms.

Appropriate for: Large established firms, firms in highly competitive markets, firms where web design is a critical business differentiator.

**What's Actually Worth the Money?**

I'm often asked: is the $20,000+ site worth it compared to the $5,000 site?

The honest answer: sometimes yes, sometimes no. It depends entirely on whether that extra investment actually improves conversion and generates more qualified leads.

A $20,000 custom site with mediocre conversion strategy will underperform a $5,000 template site that's been carefully optimized for conversion.

What you're actually paying for at higher price points is:
- Strategic thinking about conversion, not just design
- Sophisticated features that support your specific practice areas
- Ongoing optimization and A/B testing
- Integration with your business systems (CRM, calendar, email)
- Professional copywriting optimized for conversion
- Technical SEO implementation
- Advanced analytics and tracking

Those things matter. They generate real returns. But they only matter if they're done well.

The worst choice is spending $15,000 on a beautiful website that doesn't have any conversion optimization strategy built in. The best choice is understanding exactly what your firm needs to convert more visitors into clients, then paying for that—whether it's $3,000 or $30,000.

## Features That Actually Matter

Now that we've discussed cost, let's talk about what your website actually needs to do.

I've seen firms create websites with dozens of features that don't matter to potential clients, while missing the essential features that actually drive conversions.

Here are the features that meaningfully impact whether your website converts visitors into leads:

**1. Clear Value Proposition (Above the Fold)**

When someone lands on your site, they should immediately understand: What does this firm do? Who do you serve? Why should I contact you instead of a competitor?

This should be visible without scrolling. No clever headlines. No vague taglines. Something like: "Employment law representation for small business owners" or "Personal injury claims in the Phoenix area."

Your value proposition should answer the question running through every visitor's head: "Is this the right place for my legal problem?"

**2. Practice Area Pages**

Your website needs dedicated pages for each practice area your firm emphasizes. Not just a list on one page—individual pages for employment law, personal injury, family law, etc.

Each practice area page should:
- Clearly explain what this practice area covers
- Explain what problems you solve
- Include relevant case results or examples
- Have client testimonials specific to that practice area
- Have a clear CTA (call to action)

This matters because someone searching for "employment lawyer" needs to immediately see that you handle employment cases. They shouldn't have to dig through your website to figure it out.

**3. Client Testimonials and Case Results**

Trust is everything. Potential clients want evidence that other people like them hired your firm and got good results.

Your website needs real testimonials from actual clients. Video testimonials are even better. And if appropriate for your practice areas, you need case results or outcomes—specific information about cases you won or settlements you achieved.

Without these, potential clients assume you either don't have results to share or you're hiding them.

**4. About/Team Pages That Actually Connect**

Partner bios that list education and credentials are fine, but they're not what converts. What converts is showing that you understand clients' problems and genuinely care about solving them.

Your attorney bios should explain:
- Why you got into law
- What you specialize in
- Specific experience relevant to clients' concerns
- What clients say about working with you

Include professional photos. Real photos. Not generic stock images of lawyers in suits. Potential clients want to know who they'll actually be working with.

**5. Clear Contact Path**

This seems obvious, but you'd be shocked how many law firm websites make it difficult to actually contact the firm.

Your phone number should be:
- On every page header
- Clickable on mobile (tel: links)
- Large and prominent
- Listed multiple times throughout the site

You should have:
- A simple contact form that takes 2 minutes to complete
- Your physical address
- Hours of operation
- Expected response time ("We respond to all inquiries within 24 hours")
- Multiple contact options (phone, form, email)

The easier you make it to contact you, the more leads you'll generate.

**6. Mobile Optimization**

This is non-negotiable. Over 50% of law firm website traffic is mobile. If your site doesn't look professional and function perfectly on phones, you're losing leads.

This means:
- Fast load times on mobile networks
- Readable text without zooming
- Touchable buttons and links
- Easy-to-tap phone numbers and contact forms
- No intrusive pop-ups that block content

**7. SEO Foundation**

Your website should be built with SEO best practices from the ground up. This includes:
- Fast load speed
- Mobile responsiveness
- Clean URL structure
- Proper heading structure
- Internal linking strategy
- Schema markup for local business information
- Meta descriptions for every page
- Sitemap and robots.txt

A poorly optimized website makes it nearly impossible to rank for keywords your potential clients are searching for.

**8. Blog or Resources Section**

A blog isn't a nice-to-have. It's essential for law firm websites. Here's why:

1. It gives you content to optimize for keywords potential clients are searching for
2. It demonstrates expertise in your practice areas
3. It provides reasons for people to visit your site repeatedly
4. It gives you material to share on social media
5. It signals to search engines that your site is active and regularly updated

You don't need to publish three times a week. But monthly or biweekly posts on relevant legal topics are essential.

**9. Forms with Minimal Friction**

If you use forms to capture leads, they should be as simple as possible. Name, email, phone number, brief description of their issue. That's it.

Every additional field on a form reduces submission rates. If you need more information, you can get it on the phone.

**10. Analytics and Conversion Tracking**

You need to know what's actually happening on your website. Which pages do people visit? Where do they drop off? What percentage of visitors complete a contact form or call?

This requires:
- Google Analytics 4 properly set up
- Conversion goals defined and tracked
- Phone call tracking (if you want to measure calls from the website)
- Form submission tracking
- Regular review of this data

If you're not tracking this, you have no idea whether your website is actually working.

## Common Website Mistakes That Kill Conversions

I've reviewed enough law firm websites to identify patterns in what doesn't work. Here are the most common conversion killers:

**Mistake #1: Making Visitors Guess Your Specialization**

A website that says "We handle all areas of law" tells visitors nothing. Employment law clients don't know if you handle employment cases. Personal injury clients don't know if they're in the right place.

Specialization is a conversion tool. Pick 2-4 practice areas you really focus on and make them obvious on your site.

**Mistake #2: Burying the Phone Number**

I've seen law firm websites where you have to click through three pages to find a phone number. Some hide it in tiny text in the footer.

Your phone number should be huge, clickable, and on every single page. Make it easy for interested prospects to call.

**Mistake #3: Missing Trust Signals**

Potential clients don't know you. They're skeptical. They need reassurance that you're legitimate, experienced, and good at what you do.

Trust signals include:
- Client testimonials
- Case results
- Bar association memberships
- Certifications
- Publications
- Speaking engagements
- Years in practice
- Number of cases handled

Most law firm websites are missing most of these. Include them prominently.

**Mistake #4: Outdated Content**

A website with a blog post from 2019 looks abandoned. Testimonials that are years old feel stale. If your site looks like it hasn't been touched in five years, prospects wonder if your firm is still active.

Update your site regularly. Refresh testimonials. Add new case results. Publish new articles. Show that you're current and engaged.

**Mistake #5: Slow Load Times**

A slow website kills conversions. Research shows that every additional second of load time reduces conversion rates by 7%.

Optimize images, minimize code, use a content delivery network, and test your load speed regularly. Target load times under 3 seconds.

**Mistake #6: Auto-Playing Video or Music**

This was trendy five years ago. It's still a terrible idea. Auto-playing media makes your site feel unprofessional, startles visitors, and is annoying on mobile.

If you want video on your site, make it something people choose to play, not something that plays automatically.

**Mistake #7: No Mobile Optimization**

This can't be said enough. If your website doesn't look great on phones, it won't convert. Over half your traffic is mobile.

Test your site on actual phones, not just your browser's mobile simulator. How does it look? How fast does it load? Can you easily tap buttons and links?

**Mistake #8: Generic Stock Photos**

Using generic stock photos of lawyers in suits damages credibility. Use real photos of your actual team, your actual office, and actual clients (with permission).

Real photos are more trustworthy and more engaging than stock images.

**Mistake #9: No Clear Call to Action**

Every page should have a clear CTA. What should visitors do next? Call? Fill out a form? Schedule a consultation? Don't make them guess.

Use clear language: "Call us for a free consultation" or "Schedule your consultation" or "Get your free case evaluation." Not vague buttons that say "Learn More" or "Contact Us."

**Mistake #10: No Response Time Expectation**

If someone fills out your form, they don't know when to expect a response. They wait. They get impatient. They contact a competitor.

Set expectations: "We respond to all inquiries within 24 hours" or "You'll hear from us by the next business day." Then meet those expectations.

## Website + SEO: The Technical Foundation

A beautiful, conversion-optimized website that nobody can find isn't worth much. This is where SEO comes in.

I'm not going to give you a full SEO guide here (check out our [law firm SEO guide](/guide/law-firm-seo/) for that). But your website needs to be built on a solid SEO foundation from day one.

**Technical SEO Basics**

Your website should include:

**1. Fast Load Speed**

Page speed is a ranking factor. More importantly, it impacts conversion rates. Optimize images, minimize code, use a content delivery network, and test regularly.

**2. Mobile Responsiveness**

Google prioritizes mobile-first indexing. Your site must work perfectly on phones. This isn't optional.

**3. Clean URL Structure**

URLs should be readable and include relevant keywords when possible. `/about-our-team` is better than `/page12847`. `/employment-law` is better than `/services?id=employment`.

**4. Proper Heading Structure**

Use H1, H2, H3 tags correctly to show content hierarchy. One H1 per page. Use headings to structure your content logically.

**5. Schema Markup**

Implement schema markup for local business information, attorney credentials, and practice areas. This helps search engines understand your content and can improve how your site appears in search results.

**6. Internal Linking**

Link between related pages on your site. This helps search engines understand your content structure and distributes page authority throughout your site.

**7. Sitemap and Robots.txt**

Include an XML sitemap that lists all your pages. Include a robots.txt file that tells search engines which pages to crawl.

**8. SSL Certificate**

Your site must use HTTPS, not HTTP. This is a ranking factor and a trust signal.

**9. Meta Descriptions**

Write compelling meta descriptions for every page. These appear in search results and should encourage clicks.

**10. Page Titles**

Write clear, keyword-relevant page titles. Include your location if you serve a specific area.

**Beyond Technical SEO**

Technical SEO creates the foundation, but what actually generates rankings are:

- **Quality content** that answers questions your potential clients are asking
- **Relevant keywords** naturally incorporated into your content
- **Backlinks** from other websites pointing to your site
- **Local SEO** optimization if you serve a specific geographic area

Your website should make it easy for search engines to crawl and understand your content. But ultimately, you'll need an ongoing SEO strategy to generate meaningful visibility.

## Conversion Optimization: Turning Visitors Into Consultations

You can have the most beautiful website in the world. If visitors don't actually contact you or call you, it doesn't matter.

Conversion optimization is the science of turning website visitors into leads and clients. Here's how to do it:

**1. Clarity Over Everything Else**

The number one principle of conversion optimization is clarity. Every element of your site should clearly communicate one thing.

Your homepage should clearly state what you do and who you serve. Your practice area pages should clearly explain what problems you solve. Your contact page should clearly show how to reach you.

When visitors are confused, they leave. When they're clear, they convert.

**2. Social Proof and Trust Signals**

People are more likely to take action when they see other people like them have taken the same action successfully.

Use client testimonials extensively. Include before-and-after case results. Show the number of cases you've handled. Display certifications and memberships.

Video testimonials are particularly powerful. A real client saying "Keith and his team completely changed my case" is far more convincing than any marketing copy you could write.

**3. The Value Proposition**

You need to immediately communicate why someone should choose your firm instead of a competitor.

This might be:
- "We handle employment cases in tech companies and understand your industry"
- "Free case evaluation with no obligation"
- "Specialists in family law with 25 years combined experience"
- "We recover an average of $X in settlement cases"

Whatever it is, communicate it upfront.

**4. Reducing Friction**

Every step required to contact you is an opportunity for someone to drop off.

Minimize the friction:
- Phone number everywhere and easy to call from mobile
- Simple contact form (3-5 fields max)
- Multiple contact options (phone, form, email, chat)
- Fast response times
- Clear next steps

**5. Testing and Iteration**

Conversion optimization isn't a one-time thing. It's ongoing.

Test different headlines. A/B test different CTA button colors. Track which pages convert and which don't. Gradually improve your conversion rate over time.

Even small improvements add up. If you increase your conversion rate from 2% to 3%, that's a 50% increase in leads from the same traffic.

**6. Addressing Objections**

Visitors have objections running through their head:
- "Is this the right firm for my case?"
- "Will this be expensive?"
- "Can I trust this firm?"
- "How long will this take?"

Address these objections directly on your site. FAQs are perfect for this. A page about your fee structure. Testimonials about responsiveness. A timeline of how cases typically progress.

When you address their unspoken concerns, conversions increase.

## Choosing a Web Design Partner

Most law firms don't have the time or expertise to build their website in-house. You'll probably hire someone.

Here's how to choose wisely and avoid hiring someone who'll waste your money.

**Questions to Ask Potential Designers**

1. **Do you have experience with law firm websites specifically?**

This matters. Someone who's built 50 law firm websites understands the conversion challenges and what works. Someone whose experience is all e-commerce or SaaS might not.

2. **Can you show examples of sites you've built that actually generate leads?**

Anyone can show you a beautiful website. Ask specifically for examples of sites built for law firms that were designed to convert. Ask how their previous clients' conversion rates changed after the redesign.

3. **What's your approach to conversion optimization?**

If they say "we focus on design and it looks great," that's a red flag. They should be talking about conversion strategy, user psychology, and testing.

4. **How do you approach SEO?**

Will the site be built with SEO best practices? Do they do keyword research? Will they implement schema markup and optimize for search?

5. **What's included in the project scope and timeline?**

Be clear about what you're getting. How many pages? How many revision rounds? What about photography, copywriting, and content creation? How long will it actually take?

6. **How will you measure success?**

What metrics will you track? How will you know if the website is actually working? A good designer should be eager to measure results.

**Red Flags**

Avoid designers who:
- Focus only on design without discussing conversion
- Can't show you examples of their previous work
- Quote extremely low prices (usually reflects low quality)
- Don't ask about your goals and target audience
- Won't commit to response time or support after launch
- Haven't worked with law firms before
- Don't mention SEO or mobile optimization
- Make vague promises ("we'll get you lots of leads")

**Good Signs**

Look for designers who:
- Have law firm experience specifically
- Ask detailed questions about your target clients and goals
- Can show before-and-after conversion metrics from previous clients
- Explain their process clearly
- Include strategy and conversion optimization, not just design
- Commit to timeline and ongoing support
- Understand SEO
- Can explain why they're recommending specific design decisions

**The Relationship After Launch**

Your relationship with your web designer shouldn't end at launch. The best designers will:
- Monitor analytics and conversion rates
- Suggest ongoing optimizations based on data
- Help you with regular content updates
- Fix issues quickly
- Adapt the site as your business needs change

If your designer goes dark after launch, that's a problem. You need an ongoing partner, not just someone to build something once.

## Getting Started: Your Next Steps

If you've read this far, you probably recognize that your current website might not be working as hard as it should be.

Here's what to do:

**Step 1: Audit Your Current Website**

Spend an hour on your site. Look at it from the perspective of a potential client with a legal problem:
- Can I immediately tell what this firm does?
- Can I find a phone number easily?
- Does this firm handle my specific legal issue?
- Do I see evidence that they're good at what they do (results, testimonials)?
- How easy is it to actually contact them?

Write down problems you identify.

**Step 2: Identify Your Conversion Bottleneck**

Which problem is losing you the most leads? Is it:
- Unclear specialization?
- Missing trust signals?
- Hard to contact?
- Slow load times?
- Poor mobile experience?
- Outdated look?

Fix your biggest problem first. That's where your ROI will be highest.

**Step 3: Set a Budget and Timeline**

Based on the pricing framework earlier, what are you willing to invest? How quickly do you need results?

Be realistic. A $2,000 investment will get you a redesign. A $10,000 investment will get you strategy and optimization. A $25,000 investment will get you comprehensive conversion strategy.

**Step 4: Find the Right Partner**

Use the criteria I provided earlier. Look at portfolios. Ask hard questions about conversion. Get references from previous law firm clients.

Don't hire based on price alone. Hire based on whether they understand law firm conversion.

**Step 5: Measure Results**

Once your new site launches, measure what matters:
- How many people visit?
- What's your conversion rate (visitors who contact you)?
- How many of those contacts become clients?
- How much are those clients worth to your firm?

If these metrics are improving, your investment is working. If not, it's time to diagnose what's wrong and optimize further.

**The Bottom Line**

Your law firm website is one of your most important business assets. If it's not working, that's not a small problem. That's costing you leads, clients, and revenue every single day.

The good news is that most conversion problems are fixable. Clearer messaging, better trust signals, easier contact paths, and optimized design can meaningfully improve your results.

Start with an honest audit. Identify your biggest problem. Fix it. Measure the results. Keep improving.

That's how you turn a beautiful website that generates zero leads into a revenue-generating machine that actually grows your firm.

## FAQ

**Q: How long does it take to build a custom law firm website?**

A: Timeline varies based on complexity. A basic custom site typically takes 4-8 weeks. A mid-tier site with strategy and optimization might take 8-12 weeks. Complex custom sites can take 12-16 weeks. Most of that time is planning, revisions, and testing—not actual development.

**Q: Do I need a blog?**

A: Yes. A blog serves multiple purposes: it gives you content to rank for relevant keywords, demonstrates expertise, provides social media content, and shows that your site is active. You don't need to post constantly—monthly or biweekly is fine—but blogs are essential for law firm visibility.

**Q: Should I use a template site or hire someone custom?**

A: If you're a solo practitioner just starting out, a template can work short-term. If you're serious about generating leads, a custom site with conversion optimization will significantly outperform a template. Template sites look like template sites—clients notice.

**Q: What if I can't afford a $15,000 website right now?**

A: Start with a $3,000-5,000 custom basic site from a freelancer or small agency. Get the fundamentals right: clear messaging, client testimonials, and easy contact path. You can enhance it later as your budget allows.

**Q: How do I know if my website is actually converting?**

A: Set up Google Analytics 4 and define conversion goals (form submissions, phone calls, etc.). Track what percentage of visitors convert. For law firms, 1-3% is typical. Below 1% suggests conversion problems. Above 3% is excellent.

**Q: How often should I update my website?**

A: Add fresh content (blog posts, case results, testimonials) monthly or biweekly. Review and refresh your main pages annually. Update team bios and contact information as needed. Regular updates signal that your firm is active.

**Q: Can I update the website myself after it's built?**

A: This depends on how it's built. A well-designed custom site should include a content management system (CMS) that you can use to update pages and add blog posts without coding knowledge. Ask potential designers about this.

**Q: Should I invest in professional copywriting?**

A: Yes, if you can afford it. Professional copywriting optimized for your target clients and designed to drive conversions will significantly improve conversion rates. Many designers aren't skilled copywriters. This is worth hiring separately.
